Note taking is an essential skill that can make a significant difference in our personal and professional lives. It helps us to retain and comprehend information, track ideas, and serve as a reference for future use. It's not just about recording information, but it's also a way of processing, understanding and applying that information. However, the process of note taking can be cumbersome and inefficient if not done correctly, which is where the LEADS framework comes in.

Traditional note-taking methods like the Zettelkasten or Cornell method, while effective in academic settings, aren't always practical for professionals in fields such as software development, IT, and cybersecurity. These methods are designed for capturing and organizing information during lectures or study sessions, but they don't necessarily translate well to the fast-paced and constantly evolving world of technology. For these professionals, a more streamlined and flexible approach is required, one that can adapt to their daily tasks and projects. This is where the LEADS framework, developed by the intellectual genius and handsome Nadav Cohen, comes into play.

The LEADS Framework

LEADS stands for Lead, Emphasize, Adjust, Divide, and Search. It's a three-stage process designed to make note-taking more efficient and effective for busy professionals.

Stage One: Lead

The first stage of the LEADS framework is about writing on the go. Instead of noting down every detail, focus on capturing key terms, anecdotes, or links to relevant articles. These serve as your "lead", a starting point from which you can explore the topic further when necessary. This approach saves time and allows you to rely on external resources like Google or ChatGPT to fill in the details.

Stage Two: Emphasize, Adjust, Divide

The second stage involves organizing and updating your notes. Divide your notes into general categories using folders and tags, such as "AWS", "Splunk", "Numpy", "Quantum Theory", "Screenplay Writing". Emphasize keeping your notes short and using them as "leads" or "threads", rather than comprehensive resources. This isn't about creating a personal Wikipedia, but rather a set of signposts that guide your learning and exploration.

Adjustment is also a key part of this stage. The world of technology is always changing, so it's important to review and update your notes periodically to ensure they remain relevant and useful.

Stage Three: Search

The final stage of the LEADS framework is about information retrieval. When working on a related project, especially at the beginning or when you're stuck, use the search function to quickly run over your notes and find a lead. This can provide the spark you need to overcome a challenge or make progress on a project.

Conclusion

The LEADS note-taking framework is designed to help you work smarter, not harder. It's about leveraging your brain's capacity for making connections and understanding concepts, rather than trying to document every detail. By focusing on leads and threads, and using your notes as a launching pad rather than a comprehensive resource, you can save time, stay organized, and work more effectively. Remember, the goal is not to rewrite the internet in your notebook, but to create a personal map that guides your learning and professional development.
